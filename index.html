01
The premise

A decision model, not a sales model

The model’s job is not to make the programme look good. It is to answer one question honestly: can a paid Gnosis Pay card programme make commercial and operational sense for Gleec once every real cost is counted?

“Every real cost” means provider fees, Visa costs, fraud and FinCrime operations, support headcount, acquisition spend and collateral — all included before the answer is read. The model is fair precisely because it does not force a positive result.

In the earlier version, at the current $7 acquisition cost, the answer was uncomfortable: break-even only in month 18, cumulatively negative across the two years. This updated model reprices the customer top-up fee from a break-even 0.25% to 1.75% — turning top-up from a neutral pass-through into the largest single revenue line. With that one change, break-even moves to month 5, the early losses are recovered by month 9, and the two years finish +$2.2M cumulatively.

Gnosis Pay is treated as the card-programme infrastructure provider. Gleec keeps ownership of its user relationship, support intake, governance, evidence quality and residual business risk.

04
Revenue

How the model earns money

Two lines now carry the model. The repriced top-up fee at 1.75% is the single largest source — $300k/mo at month 24 — and the monthly card subscription is the stable base: three paid tiers ($3 / $9 / $15) blending at 65 / 25 / 10 to a weighted $5.70 per active card.

Interchange is regulated-aware: a 0.47% gross pool, on which Gleec earns Gnosis’s revenue share in marginal tiers — 50% up to $5M monthly volume, 70% from $5M–$20M, 75% above. The customer FX markup has been dropped to 0% (base), so FX is now negligible; ATM rounds out the mix. The customer is not charged for virtual card issuance.

The swing is almost entirely the top-up line. It hangs on one commercial judgement: will users accept a 1.75% fee to load their card? Top-up is high-friction — people will pay to spend but resent paying to fund. If churn stays low at 1.75%, the programme is strongly cash-generative. If the fee has to come down to protect activation, the result lands somewhere between these two columns. CAC discipline (holding the $7) remains the second lever.

07
Liquidity

The number that actually constrains us

Now that the programme turns cash-positive so early, the liquidity picture is almost purely collateral. The 5-day security deposit climbs to $2.5M by month 24; the only operating drag is a shallow −$102k dip in the first four months, recovered by month 9. So the peak cash need is ~$2.5M — essentially all collateral. The accounting cost of that deposit is small; the lock-up of the cash is the real constraint.

08
Operating layer

Lean, but real

The model does not pretend Gleec builds an issuer-grade fraud department on day one. Gnosis owns the core controls — KYC/AML, sanctions, transaction monitoring, fraud tooling, card blocking, chargebacks, disputes and scheme processes. Gleec owns the customer relationship: Level-1 intake, ticket quality, evidence collection, escalation, governance and residual risk.

Support scales at 1 analyst / 3,000 active cards — about 10 analysts by month 24. As volume grows, disputes and fraud escalation should stop being informal favours and become defined roles with SLA tracking.

Before any commercial decision

What must be confirmed with Gnosis

Most open questions won’t destroy the model — but several can materially improve or worsen it. These are the ones to close before launch.

TOP-UP

Will users accept a 1.75% top-up fee?

The entire upside in this version rests here. Top-up is high-friction — a fee this size could depress activation or drive churn. Provider cost is 0.25%, so the net margin is 1.50%, but only if the fee holds. This is the single most important commercial assumption to validate.

FX

FX markup set to 0%

This version drops the customer FX markup to 0% (base), so FX is no longer a revenue line. Reinstating even a small markup later is pure upside. Confirm Gleec can operationally set FX / ATM markups without contract friction.

KYC

KYC cost — $3 vs $0.50

KYC is a major onboarding cost. The $0.50 figure likely refers to token sharing, not full identity checks. Reconfirm with Ari; the model must not conflate the two. Currently $3 per new user.

SLA

Fraud & dispute SLA documents

Needed before public launch — they shape support design and residual risk. Open launch-readiness flag.

CASH

Collateral days

5 days creates a $2.5M lock-up at M24 — now the dominant liquidity need. Sensitivity also shows 3 and 4 days — a direct lever on peak cash.

CARD

Physical card economics

Issuance, fulfilment, delivery and inventory risk. Deliberately excluded from the base case; revisit only with its own model.

—
Bottom line

What the decision actually is

On this version’s assumptions the programme is no longer a strategic bet — it is cash-generative from month 5 and finishes the two years +$2.2M. But that result is carried by one decision, so the honesty of the model now lives in one question.

The whole upside rests on the 1.75% top-up fee holding without depressing activation. If users accept it, the economics are strong and the remaining work is executional — confirm the $3 KYC cost, hold Gnosis SLA risk in check, keep CAC at $7, and manage the $2.5M collateral lock-up. If the fee has to come down to protect growth, the real answer sits between the two columns in section 6.

The model no longer asks whether the programme can work. It asks whether users will pay 1.75% to load their card — and that is a question to test with real customers, not to settle in a spreadsheet.
